Stage 1 Awareness — making the user feel found

The quiz opens before the user has made any commitment. No account, no payment, no promise. Just a question. And it's the right question — broad enough to include almost everyone, specific enough to feel personal.

What I noticed

"Do you often feel tired and low on energy throughout the day?"

I answered before I finished reading. There was no hesitation — just instant recognition. That's the awareness hook: not a feature, not a benefit, but a feeling the user already carries. The quiz doesn't introduce a problem. It names one that already exists.

The funnel logic at this stage

At the Awareness stage, the goal is a single thing: make the right person feel seen. Ruut's opening questions are designed to achieve exactly that — fatigue, concentration, brain fog, decision-making. Each one is broad enough to resonate widely, and personal enough that the user thinks "this is about me." That's how you earn the next click.

Stage 2 Acquisition — deepening the investment

Once the user is in, the quiz shifts format. Questions become first-person statements. The user is no longer answering about themselves — they are agreeing with something already written about them. That's a different kind of engagement.

What I noticed

"I often wake up feeling tired no matter how much sleep I got."

When the format changed to first-person statements rated 1 to 5, I felt the shift. Before, I was describing myself. Now I was recognizing myself. The emotional weight increased. Each statement added to a growing sense that this product understands something real about my life — and that made me want to keep going.

Trust at the right moment

Stanford, Cambridge, Harvard — placed mid-quiz, not at the start

Scientific credibility appeared not as an opening sales argument, but at the precise moment when a first-time user might quietly wonder: is this real? The timing answers a question before it becomes doubt. That's a deliberate acquisition mechanic — keeping the user moving forward by removing friction exactly where it could appear.

The funnel logic at this stage

Acquisition here isn't about getting an email or a payment — it's about getting time and attention. Every additional minute the user spends in the quiz is an investment. The format shift to first-person statements deepens that investment emotionally. By the time the quiz ends, the user has committed far more than a click. They've committed their story.

Stage 3 Activation — the moment everything lands

The quiz ends with a screen that does something specific: it turns the user's answers into a visual identity. Not a report. Not a score on a spreadsheet. A figure. A battery. A number that means something.

What I noticed

Body Battery LOW — a feeling given a shape

The result screen showed a figure hunched forward with a near-empty battery above his head. Sleep Debt over 5 hours. Energy Level: Low. After answering every question honestly, seeing yourself reflected back as that figure is something else entirely. It's not abstract data — it's a feeling I already knew, now given a name and a visual form. That's the activation moment: the user doesn't just understand their problem, they feel it differently.

The funnel logic at this stage

Activation is the "aha moment" — the point where a user understands the product's value clearly enough to act. Ruut's Body Battery screen creates that moment visually and emotionally, not just informationally. The email ask that follows doesn't feel like a gate. It feels like the natural next step in a journey the user already decided to take.

"The quiz doesn't ask for the email. By the time it appears, the user is already looking for it."

Stage 4 Retention — what the product promises next

Beyond the quiz, the Ruut site outlines what the product looks like after signup. Three elements stand out from a retention perspective.

🎯
Custom Plans
Quiz data — body battery, sleep debt, HRV — becomes a personalized daily program. The product continues what the quiz started.
⏱️
10 Minutes a Day
A low enough commitment to start. Many users report changes within the first week — fast enough to keep the habit alive.
🔁
Daily Return
"Log in daily and follow your plan." The retention mechanic is simple: make coming back feel like progress.

The funnel logic at this stage

Retention depends on one thing: does the product deliver the feeling the quiz created? The quiz built a specific expectation — that this program was made for this person, based on their answers. If that personalization continues to feel real in daily use, the user stays. The first week is likely where that question gets answered.
